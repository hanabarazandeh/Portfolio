Overview

University enrolment is an Observable tool with a series of visualizations that I designed and developed to explore post secondary enrolment patterns in Canada.

Vis 2: International university enrolment

For readers convenience, I will only introduce the visualization that I find the most important in the project:

Vis 2: International university enrolment by country of citizenship, gender, and field of study, throughout the years 2010-2018

In this interactive visualization I intended to explore the change in the population of international students for each region as well as the STEM vs BHASE ratio for each dimension.

The reason why I chose to analyze so many dimensions together in one vis was is that it raises questions that I made other visualizations to explore.

Since this is a complex data to deal with, I decided on a highly interactive vis to enable exploration across different subsets.

Below you can see the structure that I decided on for vis 2:

Next decision I needed to make was the visual encodings:

I decided to choose visual encodings that are easiest to comprehend for human brain, so that it does not add to the complexity of this visualization; we are the most accurate at mapping length to a value in our brains according to Stevens power law.

So I decided on bar chart for both charts.

And for the second visual encoding I chose color, because it is best for distinguishing between discrete categories.

Challenges

The main challenge I faced was finding the appropriate data that would allow me to explore the questions arose by vis 2.

For instance, For visualization 5, I wanted to examine the academic performances of regions with high STEM ratio in female students (Middle East and Central and Southern Asia ). But I could not find any data on that.

So instead, I used academic performance in high school for East Asian countries, which still answered the question: there is no correlation between academic performance and choosing STEM.

What I learnt

After hours and hours of looking for datasets, I learnt a valuable lesson:

As designers, we won't always necessary have the perfect data, or the most solid idea given by the client to work with. And that is what makes the design process a beautiful experience: to take something complex or vague and make it simple and easy to understand.
